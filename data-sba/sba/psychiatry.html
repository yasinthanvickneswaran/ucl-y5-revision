A 44-year-old man with well-controlled bipolar disorder presents with a 6-month history of polyuria and polydipsia. He drinks large volumes of water and wakes several times nightly to pass urine. He was recently admitted to hospital for dizziness and received intravenous 0.9% sodium chloride for presumed dehydration with no improvement in symptoms. His regular medications include lithium and sertraline. On examination, he appears euvolaemic with moist mucous membranes and capillary refill less than 2 seconds. Investigations: Serum sodium 126 mmol/L, Serum osmolality 268 mOsm/kg, Urine osmolality 150 mOsm/kg, Urine sodium 30 mmol/L, Serum lithium 1.1 mmol/L, eGFR 75 mL/min/1.73m². A water deprivation test shows less than 200 mOsm/kg and after desmopressin, urine osmolality increases to 185 mOsm/kg. What is the most appropriate management?
  • A. Start hydrochlorothiazide
  • B. Reduce lithium dose and re-titrate
  • C. Start intranasal desmopressin
  • D. Stop sertraline and institute fluid restriction
  • E. Start amiloride
Amiloride is preferred as first line according to some due to it inhibiting the cellular uptake of lithium from distal tubules and collecting ducts. https://www.openaccessjournals.com/articles/management-and-treatment-of-lithiuminduced-nephrogenic-diabetes-insipidus.pdf
