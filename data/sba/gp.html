  • E. The number of prescriptions needed per patient per year
NNT (number needed to treat) is the reciprocal of the absolute risk reduction (ARR). For example, if a drug reduces 10-year MI risk from 10% to 8%, the ARR is 2% and the NNT is 1/0.02 = 50. What does 'p-value < 0.05' mean in the context of clinical trial results?
  • A. There is a 95% chance the treatment works
  • B. The treatment is clinically significant
  • C. There is less than a 5% probability that the observed result occurred by chance alone — the result is statistically significant
  • D. The study enrolled fewer than 5% of eligible patients
  • E. The trial ran for less than five years
A p-value represents the probability of observing results at least as extreme as those obtained, assuming the null hypothesis (no effect) is true. A p-value < 0.05 means there is less than a 5% probability the result occurred by chance — it is statistically significant by conventional thresholds. Importantly, statistical significance does not equal clinical significance — a large study may find a statistically significant but clinically trivial difference. GPs must interpret results in context.

A GP researcher wants to pool the results of ten RCTs examining the effect of exercise on depression. What statistical method would best achieve this?
  • A. Narrative review
  • B. Systematic review without meta-analysis
  • C. Meta-analysis
  • D. Cross-sectional survey
  • E. Case-control study
Meta-analysis is a statistical technique that pools quantitative results from multiple studies (ideally identified through a systematic review), increasing statistical power and precision of effect estimates. For ten RCTs all examining the same intervention-outcome pair (exercise on depression), meta-analysis would produce an overall estimate of effect size with confidence intervals. Forest plots visually display the results of each study and the pooled estimate.

Which of the following best describes the purpose of a 'confidence interval' in clinical research?
  • A. It indicates how confident the researcher feels about their hypothesis
  • B. It shows the range within which the true population value likely lies, with a specified probability (usually 95%)
  • C. It measures the sample size needed for a study
  • D. It indicates the level of peer review the study received
  • E. It shows the statistical significance of a result
A 95% confidence interval (CI) is a range of values within which the true population effect is estimated to lie with 95% probability. A narrow CI indicates a precise estimate; a wide CI reflects uncertainty (often due to small sample size). If a 95% CI for a treatment effect crosses zero (for risk differences) or one (for odds ratios/risk ratios), the result is not statistically significant at the p < 0.05 level. CIs are more informative than p-values alone.
