A 29-year-old woman at 11 weeks of pregnancy presents with a 3-week history of intractable nausea and vomiting. She has been unable to tolerate any oral intake and has lost 4 kg. She is admitted and commenced on intravenous 5% dextrose at 125 mL/hour. After 48 hours, the nursing staff note she appears confused and is having difficulty walking. On examination, she is disorientated to time and place, with bilateral lateral gaze restriction and an unsteady wide-based gait. Her observations are stable and urinalysis is unremarkable. What is the most likely explanation for her new neurological findings?
  • A. Hyperemesis gravidarum causing direct neurological toxicity
  • B. Hyponatraemia secondary to prolonged vomiting
  • C. Depletion of a micronutrient precipitated by the adminstered fluid
  • D. Intracranial hypertension from fluid overload
  • E. Uraemia encephalopathy from acute kidney injury
Answer: C — Depletion of a micronutrient precipitated by the administered fluid The clinical triad of confusion, ophthalmoplegia (lateral gaze palsy), and ataxia is Wernicke's encephalopathy. This patient has had prolonged vomiting with zero oral intake, making her thiamine (vitamin B1) stores critically low. Administering glucose-containing fluid without thiamine replacement is the precipitant: glucose metabolism is entirely thiamine-dependent, and a large glucose load acutely exhausts residual stores, unmasking the deficiency. This is a well-recognised danger of giving dextrose to malnourished or at-risk patients without first giving parenteral thiamine. Why not the others? Hyponatraemia (B) can cause confusion and even gait disturbance, but does not classically cause a gaze palsy — and no electrolytes are provided to suggest this. Fluid overload causing raised intracranial pressure (D) would be expected to produce headache, papilloedema, and bradycardia, not this specific triad. Uraemic encephalopathy (E) is inconsistent with stable observations and a normal urinalysis. Option A is a distractor — hyperemesis itself does not directly cause neurological injury.

A 26-year-old woman at 18 weeks of gestation presents to the emergency department with a 2-day history of a widespread vesicular rash, fever, and increasing breathlessness. On examination, her oxygen saturation is 91% on air, respiratory rate is 28 breaths per minute, and she has widespread vesicular lesions across her trunk and face. A chest X-ray shows bilateral infiltrates. What is the most appropriate immediate management?
  • A. Oral aciclovir 800 mg five times daily and discharge with safety netting
  • B. Oral valaciclovir and outpatient respiratory review in 48 hours
  • C. Intravenous aciclovir and hospital admission with respiratory monitoring
  • D. Varicella-zoster immunoglobulin (VZIG) and oral aciclovir
  • E. Supportive treatment only, as antivirals are not licensed in pregnancy
Oral aciclovir is appropriate for uncomplicated chickenpox in pregnancy when presenting within 24 hours of rash onset at ≥20 weeks. However, this patient has clinical features of severe varicella pneumonia — hypoxia, tachypnoea, and bilateral infiltrates — which is a well-recognised and potentially life-threatening complication of chickenpox in adults. The guideline is unambiguous that intravenous aciclovir should be given to all pregnant women with severe chickenpox, and hospital admission with appropriate monitoring is mandatory. VZIG (D) has no therapeutic benefit once chickenpox has developed and should not be used. Oral antiviral alone (A/B) is insufficient for severe disease. Option E is dangerous; aciclovir can be prescribed in the best interest of the woman on the basis of available evidence, despite lacking a formal pregnancy licence.
A 32-year-old woman at 14 weeks of gestation is seen in the antenatal clinic. She reports that her 4-year-old son was diagnosed with chickenpox 3 days ago and she has been living in the same household throughout. She has no recollection of having chickenpox as a child and has not been vaccinated. She has no rash. Her booking bloods are available. What is the most appropriate next step?
  • A. Reassure her and advise her to return if a rash develops
  • B. Administer VZIG immediately as she has had significant exposure
  • C. Check the stored booking blood sample for VZV IgG before deciding on management
  • D. Prescribe oral aciclovir immediately as post-exposure prophylaxis
  • E. Advise her to isolate for 21 days and arrange a follow-up appointment
The guideline states that women with an uncertain or absent history of chickenpox who have had significant exposure should have a blood test to determine VZV immunity. Crucially, the booking sample — already stored — can often provide a rapid result, sometimes within hours. At least 80% of women tested will be found to have VZV IgG and can be reassured without any intervention. Administering VZIG or antivirals (B/D) before establishing immune status is not recommended and wastes a limited, expensive resource. Reassurance alone (A) would be inappropriate given the uncertain history. Isolation advice (E) is relevant but incomplete without first establishing her immune status.
A woman at 22 weeks of gestation is confirmed to have chickenpox and develops a vesicular rash. She is otherwise well with no respiratory symptoms. She presents to her GP 20 hours after the rash first appeared. What is the most appropriate antiviral management?
  • A. No antivirals — she is beyond the window for treatment
  • B. Oral aciclovir 800 mg five times daily for 7 days
  • C. Intravenous aciclovir, as she is in the second trimester
  • D. VZIG, as it attenuates disease severity
  • E. Valaciclovir is preferred over aciclovir due to its superior safety profile in pregnancy
The guideline recommends oral aciclovir for pregnant women with chickenpox who present within 24 hours of rash onset and are at ≥20+0 weeks of gestation. This patient meets both criteria. She does not have severe disease requiring IV aciclovir (C). VZIG (D) has no therapeutic benefit once chickenpox has developed. While valaciclovir has a more convenient dosing schedule, the guideline recommends aciclovir as first choice because there is a greater body of safety data in pregnancy; valaciclovir can be considered if adherence to the aciclovir regimen is a concern. Option A is incorrect — she is within the 24-hour window.
A 28-year-old woman at 26 weeks of gestation has recovered from chickenpox, which she developed 5 weeks ago. She is now seen in the fetal medicine unit for follow-up. Her rash has fully crusted over. Which of the following most accurately describes the risk and recommended surveillance for her fetus?
  • A. There is no risk of fetal varicella syndrome as her infection occurred after 20 weeks
  • B. She should be referred for detailed ultrasound at 16–20 weeks gestation or 5 weeks after infection, whichever is later
  • C. Amniocentesis for VZV DNA by PCR should be performed routinely in all cases to exclude fetal infection
  • D. Fetal varicella syndrome can occur with maternal infection up to 28 weeks; she should be counselled about a small risk and offered detailed ultrasound
  • E. MRI is the investigation of first choice for detecting fetal varicella syndrome
The guideline states that FVS has been reported following maternal infection as late as 28 weeks, and that women who develop varicella in the first 28 weeks should be informed of the small risk (~1% before 20 weeks, with cases documented up to 28 weeks) and referred to fetal medicine. Detailed ultrasound at 5 weeks after infection (or at 16–20 weeks) is the recommended first-line investigation — not MRI (E), though MRI may provide additional information where ultrasound has already identified abnormalities. Amniocentesis (C) is not routine; the guideline advises counselling about its risks and benefits because it has a strong negative predictive value but poor positive predictive value, and should not be performed before skin lesions have completely healed.
A woman develops chickenpox at 38 weeks of gestation. Her rash appears on a Monday. She goes into spontaneous labour on the following Wednesday (2 days later) and delivers a live male infant. Regarding the management of the neonate, which statement is most accurate?
  • A. As the infant has received passive maternal antibodies transplacentally, no further intervention is required
  • B. The neonate should receive VZIG with or without aciclovir as soon as possible
  • C. The neonate should be tested for VZV IgG before any prophylaxis is given
  • D. Breastfeeding should be discontinued until the maternal lesions have fully crusted
  • E. The neonate is not at significant risk as he was born after 37 weeks
When maternal chickenpox occurs within 7 days before delivery (or up to 7 days after), there is a significant risk of severe neonatal varicella because there has been insufficient time for protective maternal antibodies to be generated and transferred. In this case, delivery occurred only 2 days after rash onset — well within the high-risk window. The guideline states there is no need to test in these circumstances; the neonate should receive VZIG with or without aciclovir promptly. Gestational age at delivery (E) is irrelevant to this risk. Breastfeeding (D) should be encouraged if the mother is well enough and there are no active lesions near the nipple — it is not contraindicated by the diagnosis itself.
